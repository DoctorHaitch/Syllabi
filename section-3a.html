    Section 4 MATH122 - Calculus 2

    This course builds on your foundation in calculus by introducing advanced integration techniques, applications of integration, improper integrals, numerical methods, calculus of parametric curves, and infinite sequences and series. These topics are core analytical tools used throughout science, engineering, and applied mathematics.
    If some of this terminology is unfamiliar, that’s expected, you’re taking this course to start learning these concepts! The first section below outlines the kinds of problems and questions these concepts help us address—from modeling physical systems to analyzing complex data. By the end of the course, you’ll have a basic understanding of how calculus might help with rigorous problem-solving in STEM fields.
    In addition to mastering these new topics, you’ll continue to reinforce fluency with essential skills from arithmetic, algebra, trigonometry, and Calculus I. These fundamentals remain critical across the sciences for precisely quantifying and interpreting relationships in real-world systems.

    Subsection 4.1 Course Outline

    A planned schedule for the semester is provided at the end of this document. Please see it for important dates such as quizzes and exams.
    Roughly, our semester is organized to study the following questions (in order):
    1. How can the relationships between integrals and derivatives help us model real-world phenomena? In general, how can integration help us calculate useful quantities? (Sections xx)
    2. What can we do when the anti-derivative is not obvious? (Sections xx)
    3. How do we work with curves that are not functions or perform Calculus in other coordinate systems? (Sections xx)
    4. How do we deal with quantities involving infinity that still result in finite answers? (Sections xx)
    5. Finally, when our techniques from answering (2) fail (or are impossible), can we still find meaningful answers to Calculus questions? If so, how? (Sections xx)

    Subsection 4.2 Why study calculus?

    This rigorous mathematical subject consists of a basic mathematical tool kit for dealing with processes that change continuously. Part of the reason that calculus turns out to be so useful is that the details of the process turn out not to matter so much -- all that matters is that the process is continuous. In fact, if the increments are small enough, discrete processes can sometimes be approximated as continuous and treated using the techniques of calculus. More specifically, Calculus is the story of infinitely improving approximations concerned with two historical problems:
    1. The slope of a "curvy line" at a point.
    2. The area beneath a "curvy line".
    Miraculously, these two problems, which may seem barely related except for the type of line they seek to study, turn out to be deeply intertwined. This semester, we will primarily focus on the second problem, but due to the dual nature of these problems the other is necessary and a skill which you hopefully developed during Calculus I.
    In order to do well in MATH122, you need to work on developing both conceptual understanding and algebraic fluency. Conceptual understanding is necessary as memorized facts are not much help if you do not know how to use them. Algebraic fluency is required as knowing the basic concepts is not very helpful if you are unable to easily use them because your knowledge is not built on a firm foundation of the governing principles.
    By fluency, I mean that you can generally use them without thinking about them. Fluency is defined as the ability to speak or write a language easily and accurately. Similar to your fluency in your native language, where you likely are fluent in 10000-20000 words, you are expected to be fluent in a number of "mathematical rules" which you have learned over the last several years.     Subsection 4.3 Prequisites

    Formally, being enrolled in MATH122 requires having passed MATH121 with a ’C’ or better, or explicit consent of the instructor. Any student being found not to meet these requirements will automatically receive a failing grade at the end of the semester.
    In practice, Calculus II depends heavily on algebra, analytic geometry, trigonometry and your knowledge from Calculus I. Several review worksheets are posted on D2L Brightspace to help you recall the knowledge that will help you succeed in Calculus II this semester.     Subsection 4.4 Learning Outcomes

    Upon completion of this course students will have demonstrated
    1. a working knowledge of Riemann integration theory and several specific applications. This will build off material from MATH121: Calculus I.
    2. a working knowledge of sequences, series, and their relevance to calculus.
    3. their ability to memorize, derive, and practice facts (the derivatives and antiderivatives of basic functions, for instance) and fundamental theorems.
    4. understanding what is going on behind the symbols by developing the patterns of thought and the problem-solving skills needed when dealing with problems involving continuous change and difficult integrals. If you limit yourself to memorizing facts and formulas, you will miss out on this very important part of the course, which makes calculus so important for so many application areas.
    5. team work skills by collaborating within a small group, towards completion of a common task or goal.

    Subsection 4.8 Suggested Textbooks

    The textbook officially adopted by the department for this course is:
    • Essential Calculus: Early Transcendentals by James Stewart, \(2^{\textrm{nd}}\) edition.
    • ISBN: 978-1133112280
    However, since the required practice will be assigned directly to you, almost any Calculus textbook will suffice. This includes any edition of Stewart’s texts, freely available Open Educational Resource (OER) options from sites like OpenStax, and others. The most important consideration is the order of topics. For example, Stewart’s textbooks typically present advanced integration techniques before the applications of integration, while this course takes the reverse approach—motivating techniques through early applications.
    A textbook that follows a similar order to this course is Calculus by Larson and Edwards (currently in its 12th edition, though any edition will work). Students looking for an affordable physical textbook might consider an older edition of Larson and Edwards.
    Another strong and inexpensive alternative closely aligned with the departmental textbook is:
    • APEX Calculus

We will primarily cover content from Calculus 2, with a few sections drawn from Calculus 3. If you prefer a printed textbook, APEX Calculus Volume 2 is likely sufficient; the few Calculus 3 topics we cover can be accessed digitally.

    Subsection 4.9 Prerequisite Knowledge for Calculus II

    This was originally written in Fall 2023, and will continue to evolve as necessary. Please let me know if you have suggested additions or revisions -- I’m happy to listen to your input or discuss this list!
    Below are specific topics I’ve found students need to scaffold their new Calculus II knowledge, and where I would expect you first encountered the topic, but you’ve likely seen each one multiple times in subsequent classes. This is much more specific than saying "Prerequisite: Completed Calculus I with a C or better". These are specific points of knowledge you need to build from. If you do not feel comfortable with the topics below, this is going to be where a lot of your potential struggle with Calculus II will likely come from. You’ve taken a good deal of mathematics to get here--and you are expected to have mastered these concepts in your prior 12+ years of mathematical training so you can apply them here fluenty and in all future classes. Math is the basic language of most sciences, and these concepts remain the foundation of many scientific fields--so they remain important outside of Calculus as well!

    Subsubsection 4.9.1 Arithmetic

    1. How to add, subtract, multiply, and divide
    2. The basic rules: distribution, commutativity, transitivity (especially of equality and inequalities)
    3. How to do all of the above with fractions
    4. How to get a common denominator
    5. How to simplify complex fractions
    6. How to multiply quickly by "skip counting"
    7. The division algorithm (Yes, long division)

    Subsubsection 4.9.2 Algebra

    1. Understand the difference between a mathematical function and a mathematical expression
    2. How to find the distance between two points on a number line
    3. How to solve for unknowns
    4. How to solve rational and root functions (and know what extraneous solutions are)
    5. How to solve systems of equations for multiple unknowns
      1. Method of Elimination or Substitution (both work, choose your favorite, knowing both is even better)
    6. How to determine the equation of a line
      1. Equations of Horizontal and Vertical lines
      2. Point-slope, slope-intercept, and two-point forms
    7. The graphs of basic polynomial functions and nth root functions, and how transformations affect them
      1. Graph of a function vs algebraic definition (Cartesian plane)
      2. Domains and ranges, including inverse functions
    8. How to factor a polynomial equation
      1. General Strategies
        1. Factor by grouping
        2. Guess and test factoring
        3. Do not rely on graphs
      2. Special Strategies for Quadratics
        1. Sum/product method
        2. Quadratic formula
        3. Discriminant and root classification
        4. Recognizing irreducible factors
        5. Perfect square and difference of squares formulas
        6. Completing the square
          Remark 4.2.
          Also, if you want to study classical physics, Newton literally wrote a book called "Conic Sections" -- because they are that important.
      3. Advanced Strategies
        1. Descartes’ Rule of Signs, Rational Root Theorem
        2. Synthetic division
    9. How to add, subtract, multiply, and divide functions
    10. Equality of functions
      1. Specifically, when polynomial functions are equal
    11. Rules of exponents
      1. And how they relate to logarithmic rules
    12. The division algorithm for polynomials
    13. The absolute value function
      1. As piecewise and radical form
      2. Graph and geometric meaning
    14. Even and Odd functions
    15. Graphs of rational functions
      1. Asymptotes and discontinuities
      2. End behavior and limits

    Subsubsection 4.9.3 Trigonometry

    1. Graphs of trig functions and transformations (translations and dilations)
      1. \(\sin x, \cos x, \tan x, \sec x, \csc x, \cot x\) (and their inverses)
    2. The unit triangle. Instead of memorizing, learn to construct it from minimal knowledge such as
      1. SOHCAHTOA and ASTC
      2. Using special triangles to evaluate trig functions at common angles
    3. Coordinates on the unit circle using \(\sin x\) and \(\cos x\)
    4. Common trig identities
      1. Reciprocal
      2. Even/Odd
      3. Pythagorean
      4. Sum of angles for \(\sin x\) and \(\cos x\)

    Subsubsection 4.9.4 Calculus I

    1. Definition of a limit and strategies to compute it
    2. Definition of derivative and its geometric meaning
      1. Equation of a tangent line
      2. First and second derivative tests
    3. Derivative rules
      1. Constant, sum/difference, product, quotient, chain
      2. Derivatives of polynomials, trig, exponential functions, and inverses
      3. Implicit differentiation
        Example: find \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) of \(y^2+3x^2 - \sin(xy) = 15\) or if \(g\) is also a function of \(x\text{,}\) calculate \(\frac{df}{dx}\) if \(f = g^2\ln(\sin(g))\text{.}\)
    4. Antiderivatives
      1. From known derivatives
      2. Memorize antiderivatives of \(\sec x\) and \(\csc x\)
      3. Algebraic manipulation
      4. \(u\)-Substitution (linked to Chain Rule)
      5. Why "\(+C\)" appears in indefinite integrals
    5. Definition of the integral as a Riemann sum limit
      1. Compute left, right, and midpoint approximations
    6. Exponential and logarithmic equations
      1. Graphs and transformations
      2. Solving and simplifying
    7. Fundamental Theorem of Calculus
      1. You don’t need to know the proof, but you should appreciate the reason Cauchy’s Mean Value Theorem is the key idea that makes this magnificent theorem work.
